What is a Near-Earth Object?

A Near-Earth Object (NEO) is any small Solar System body — asteroid or comet — whose orbit brings it within 1.3 AU (about 194 million km) of the Sun, and therefore within 0.3 AU of Earth's orbit.

As of 2024, over 34,000 NEOs have been discovered. Around 2,300 are classified as Potentially Hazardous Asteroids (PHAs).

Types of NEOs

  • Atiras — orbit entirely inside Earth's orbit
  • Atens — cross Earth's orbit, semi-major axis < 1 AU
  • Apollos — cross Earth's orbit, semi-major axis > 1 AU (largest group)
  • Amors — just outside Earth's orbit, don't cross it

How Are They Detected?

NEOs are discovered primarily by ground-based sky surveys:

  • Catalina Sky Survey (CSS) — responsible for the most NEO discoveries
  • Pan-STARRS — wide-field telescope in Hawaii
  • ATLAS — Asteroid Terrestrial-impact Last Alert System
  • NEOWISE — NASA space telescope (infrared)

What Happens During a Close Approach?

A "close approach" means an NEO passes near Earth — but rarely dangerously close. Most recorded close approaches occur at several lunar distances (LD). One LD = 384,400 km (the average Earth–Moon distance).

Potentially Hazardous Asteroids (PHAs)

An asteroid is classified as a PHA if it meets both criteria:

  • Minimum Orbit Intersection Distance (MOID) ≤ 0.05 AU (~19.5 LD)
  • Absolute magnitude H ≤ 22 (diameter roughly ≥ 140 m)
A PHA designation does not mean an impact is predicted — only that the orbital geometry makes a future close approach possible.

The Torino Scale

Used to communicate impact risk to the public, from 0 (no risk) to 10 (certain catastrophic impact).

LevelCategoryMeaning
0No HazardNo consequence. Most NEOs.
1NormalRoutine — merits monitoring.
2–4Meriting AttentionClose pass, low impact probability.
5–7ThreateningSignificant threat — national/global impact possible.
8–10Certain CollisionImpact certain. Localized to global catastrophe.

Planetary Defense

NASA's Planetary Defense Coordination Office (PDCO) tracks all known PHAs. In 2022, the DART mission successfully deflected asteroid Dimorphos by 33 minutes — proving kinetic impactor technology works.

NeoWs — NASA Near Earth Object Web Service

Provided by NASA's Center for Near Earth Object Studies (CNEOS). Returns NEO close-approach data for a specified date window.

  • Max date range: 7 days per request
  • Includes: size estimates, velocity, miss distance, PHA flag
  • Requires a free NASA API key
  • Endpoint: api.nasa.gov/neo/rest/v1/feed
Best for: daily close-approach feeds, structured data with hazard flags.

SBDB Close-Approach Data API — JPL

NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory's Small-Body Database API. More powerful than NeoWs — no 7-day limit, richer filtering, and orbital data.

  • No date range limit
  • Filter by miss distance, PHA status, body type, magnitude
  • No API key required
  • Endpoint: ssd-api.jpl.nasa.gov/cad.api
Best for: broader queries, orbital data, extended date ranges, custom filtering.

How to Get a NASA API Key

Visit api.nasa.gov and sign up for free. The key is emailed instantly and grants access to all NASA public APIs including NeoWs, APOD, Mars Rover Photos, and more. The free tier allows:

  • 1,000 requests per hour
  • 2,000 requests per day
